Importance of sleep

Sleep plays a vital role in good health and well-being throughout your life. Getting enough quality sleep at the right times can help protect your mental health, physical health, quality of life, and safety.


Healthy brain function and emotional well-being

Sleeping allows your brain to form new neural pathways to facilitate learning new skills and information.
Sleeping also heightens attention, enhances problem-solving skills and improves creativity 

Sleep defiency 

Studies shows that sleep deficiency alters activity in some areas of the brain. Being sleep deficient can cause you to have trouble making decisions, solving problems, controlling emotions and coping with change.

Mordern world context

Sleep deficiency has also been proven to be linked to depression and suicide.
Children and teens with sleep deficiency may have problems with socialising and have withdrawals. They may also be emotionally unstable and have problems focusing, resulting in lower grades and stress.  

Dangers of Sleep Deprivation
Many studies make it clear that sleep deprivation is dangerous. Sleep-deprived people who are tested by using a driving simulator or by performing a hand-eye coordination task perform as badly as or worse than those who are intoxicated.


 Sleep deprivation also magnifies alcohol's effects on the body, so a fatigued person who drinks will become much more impaired than someone who is well rested.
 Since drowsiness is the brain's last step before falling asleep, driving while drowsy can -- and often does -- lead to disaster. Caffeine and other stimulants cannot overcome the effects of severe sleep deprivation.

Physical health

Sleep is imperative for healing and repairing of your cardiovascular system.
Sleep deficiency is linked to increased risk of heart and kidney disease, high blood pressure diabetes and stroke.

Lack of sleep

Studies also show that lack of sleep increases risk of obesity. One study shows that the chances of obesity increases significantly with each hour of sleep lost.
Sleep also helps maintain a healthy balance of hormones in your body, which controls important areas such as emotions and how full you feel. 

Body development

Sleep impacts your body’s reaction to insulin. Lack of sleep results in higher blood glucose levels, hence increasing chances of hyperglycemia and diabetes.
Sleeps is crucial for healthy growth and development. Especially for teens going through puberty, sleep releases hormones which are needed for growth of their bodies such as building of muscle mass and bones and repairing of cells and tissues. 

Immune deficiency

Sleep is also important for boosting the immune system and your body’s self defence mechanism. The immune system is needed to defend against foreign bodies entering your body so that you will not get sick. However, sleep deficiency deteriorates this system, hence causing you to be more prone to illnesses.

Daytime Performance and Safety

Productivity

People who lack enough sleep are less productive at work and school. They are less efficient in completing assignments, have slower reaction times and are vulnerable to making more mistakes.

Microsleep

Sleep deficiency also engenders microsleep, a phenomenon in which you get brief moments of sleep, and these moments are uncontrollable. For example, have you listened attentively in class, but sometimes have no impression of what happened in the class at all?

AMOUNT OF SLEEP REQUIRED

HOW MUCH SLEEP ONE NEEDS TO BE WELL RESTED AND PREPARED FOR THE NEXT DAY

Pre-school children (ages 3-5)

Pre-school children (ages 3-5) require 10-13 hours a day. 

School-age children (ages 6-13) 

School-age children (ages 6-13) require 9-11 hours a day.

Teenagers (ages 14-17) 

Teenagers (ages 14-17) need about 8-10 hours each day.

Adults

Most adults need 7 to 9 hours a night for the best amount of sleep, although some people may need as few as 6 hours or as many as 10 hours of sleep each day.

Elderly

Older adults (ages 65 and older)need 7-8 hours of sleep each day.

Women with pregnancy

Women in the first 3 months of pregnancy often need several more hours of sleep than usual.
